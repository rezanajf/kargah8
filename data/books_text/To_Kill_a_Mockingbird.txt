To Kill a Mockingbird is a novel set in the American South during the Great Depression, told through the eyes of a young girl named Scout Finch. The story combines the innocence of childhood with serious social issues, especially racism and injustice. Harper Lee creates a world that is both intimate and morally challenging.

A major part of the novel involves Scout’s father, Atticus Finch, a lawyer who defends Tom Robinson, a Black man falsely accused of a crime. Through the trial and its consequences, Scout and her brother Jem come to understand the prejudice and inequality present in their community. Their experiences shape their understanding of courage, empathy, and fairness.

The novel is praised for its emotional power, moral clarity, and unforgettable characters. Atticus Finch is often seen as a symbol of integrity and compassion, while Scout’s narration gives the book honesty and warmth. To Kill a Mockingbird continues to be valued for its message about justice, humanity, and understanding others.
